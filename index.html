The Physics of Computers

by Michael Stirchak

How do Computers Work?

  • Modern computers read in electric signals as either 1's(on) or 0's(off).
  • Transistors are uniquely equipped to read in these signals, which are called bits.
  • By combining transistors in a circuit, we can construct boolean logic gates which can perform operations on bits.

MOSFETs

  • MOSFET: Metal Oxide Semiconductor Field-Effect Transistor.
  • Consists of drain, gate, and source outlets.
  • A positive voltage across the gate determines if the current from the drain passes to the source.


MOSFET Architecture

  • Drain and source feed into doped (n-type) regions of the semiconductor, and between them is a p-type region.
  • There exists a depletion layer between the n- and p-type regions which repels current.
  • When a positive voltage is across the gate, a conduction channel is formed from drain to source.


Example: MOSFET Switch

  • When Switch is open, no voltage across gate. No current flows from drain to source, and lamp (M) is off.
  • When switch is closed, the voltage across the gate allows current to flow. Lamp turns on.

MOSFET Drawbacks

There is a minimum voltage that must be across the gate for the MOSFET to function, called the Threshold Voltage. However, even when there is no voltage, there will still be some small current from drain to source.


Some Other Problems

  • Scalability: modern MOSFETs can be made ~20nm across before quantum effects occur.
  • MOSFETs have a specific operational temperature range, so integrated circuits must be cooled.

Tunneling Field-Effect Transistor (TFET)

TFETs use one of the flaws of the MOSFET, quantum tunneling, to its advantage. The gate voltage causes electrons to accumulate in the intrinsic region, allowing band-to-band tunneling to occur.


TFETs Continued

  • Superior to MOSFETs at low gate voltages, but inferior at higher gate volatges
  • Somewhat unreliable, due to unpredictable nature of quantum effects.
  • More expensive than traditional MOSFETs.

The Single Atom Transistor

A single phosphorus atom is injected onto a silicon sheet within a potential well. This dopant allows for tunneling between the drain and source.

Quantum Computing

The theory of Quantum Computing centers on the idea of state superposition. While traditional bits are either 1 or 0, a quantum computer would be able to read a qubit that is in superposition of two of its states.


Quantum Computing Continued

Such a device would have incredible benefits over traditional computers. A single qubit contains information for two possible states; two qubits contain info for four states. an N-qubit computer would have processing power equivalent to a classical computer with 2^N bits.

The problem is that qubits cannot be measured directly. The act of observing a qubit will necessitate that it exists in either its 0 or 1 state, and information about any other states it may have been in is lost.

In short, no one understands what the heck is going on down there, much less how to take advantage of it.
